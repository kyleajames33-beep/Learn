The Invisible Current

You drop sugar into your tea. You don't stir, yet five minutes later, every sip is sweet. No energy expended, no proteins involved‚Äîjust physics.

Your cells perform this same trick 2 trillion times per second, moving oxygen and nutrients without spending a single ATP molecule. But there's a catch: they can't control where it goes.

Inquiry Question: How do cells move substances without energy?

The Concentration Gradient

This diagram shows simple diffusion: the net movement of particles from an area of high concentration to low concentration.

Key Principle: This is passive‚Äîit requires no metabolic energy (ATP). The cell doesn't "do" anything; the laws of thermodynamics drive the movement.

Biological Example:

Oxygen diffuses from lungs (high O‚ÇÇ) into blood (lower O‚ÇÇ). Carbon dioxide diffuses out. Both follow their concentration gradients.

This is where facilitated diffusion bridges the gap between simple diffusion and the membrane structure.

Channel and Carrier Proteins

Large or charged molecules cannot pass through the hydrophobic core. They require facilitated diffusion‚Äîstill passive (no ATP), but using membrane proteins as tunnels or ferries.

Unit 3: What Affects the Rate?

‚ö°

Factors Affecting Diffusion Rate

1. Concentration Gradient

Steeper gradient = faster rate. As equilibrium approaches, rate slows.

2. Temperature

Higher temp = more kinetic energy = faster diffusion.

3. Surface Area

More membrane area = more sites for entry (Lesson 1 connection!).

4. Membrane Thickness

Thinner membrane = shorter distance = faster diffusion.

Deep Dive: Extensions

The classic beetroot experiment tests membrane integrity. When heated, the membrane becomes more fluid (and leaky), allowing red pigment to diffuse out. This demonstrates how temperature affects membrane structure and therefore passive transport rates.

HSC Skill: You must be able to design an experiment to test factors affecting membrane permeability.

In cystic fibrosis, the CFTR channel protein (which facilitates chloride ion diffusion) is defective. This passive transport failure leads to thick mucus‚Äîdemonstrating that even "simple" diffusion processes are critical for survival.
