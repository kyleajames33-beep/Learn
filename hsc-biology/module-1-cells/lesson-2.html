Prokaryotic Cells

Examine the structure and function of prokaryotic cells - the simplest and most ancient forms of life on Earth. Learn about bacteria and archaea, and understand what makes these cells unique.

Think About This...

Right now, your body contains more bacterial cells than human cells. Scientists estimate that around 38 trillion bacteria live in and on your body - about 1.3 bacteria for every human cell you have. Most of these are helpful, living in your gut and aiding digestion. Bacteria were the first life forms on Earth, appearing over 3.5 billion years ago. Despite being microscopic, they've shaped our planet's atmosphere, drive global nutrient cycles, and can survive in environments from boiling hot springs to frozen Antarctic ice. How can such simple cells be so successful?

What are Prokaryotes?

Prokaryotes are organisms whose cells lack a nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles. The word "prokaryote" comes from Greek: pro (before) and karyon (kernel/nucleus), meaning "before nucleus." Prokaryotes were the first cells to evolve on Earth and remain the most abundant organisms today.

Key Definition

Prokaryotic cell: A cell that lacks a membrane-bound nucleus and membrane-bound organelles. The DNA is located in a region called the nucleoid and floats freely in the cytoplasm. All prokaryotes are single-celled (unicellular) organisms.

Prokaryotes are divided into two domains of life: Bacteria and Archaea. While they share basic structural features, they differ significantly in their biochemistry, genetics, and the environments where they thrive.

Bacteria

The most familiar prokaryotes. Found everywhere on Earth - in soil, water, air, and living organisms. Most are harmless or beneficial; some cause disease.

Examples: E. coli, Streptococcus, Lactobacillus

Archaea

Often called "extremophiles" because many live in extreme environments. However, archaea also live in moderate environments including soils, oceans, and the human gut.

Examples: Methanogens, thermophiles, halophiles

Structure of a Prokaryotic Cell

Prokaryotic cells have a relatively simple structure compared to eukaryotic cells. Despite this simplicity, each component plays a vital role in the cell's survival. Let's examine each structure in detail.

Plasma Membrane (Cell Membrane)

Structure: A phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins, similar to eukaryotic membranes.

Function: Controls what enters and exits the cell (selectively permeable). Also the site of cellular respiration in prokaryotes, as they lack mitochondria.

Cell Wall

Structure: A rigid layer outside the plasma membrane. In most bacteria, it contains peptidoglycan (a polymer of sugars and amino acids). Archaeal cell walls have different compositions (often pseudopeptidoglycan or protein).

Function: Provides structural support, maintains cell shape, and protects against osmotic lysis (bursting due to water intake). The cell wall is the target of many antibiotics (e.g., penicillin).

Capsule (in some bacteria)

Structure: A sticky, gel-like layer of polysaccharides outside the cell wall. Not all bacteria have capsules.

Function: Protects against dehydration, helps bacteria stick to surfaces (biofilm formation), and helps pathogenic bacteria evade the immune system. Encapsulated bacteria are often more virulent (disease-causing).

Nucleoid

Structure: A region in the cytoplasm where the single, circular chromosome is located. Unlike a nucleus, the nucleoid has no membrane surrounding it.

Function: Contains the cell's main genetic information (DNA) that controls all cell activities and is passed to daughter cells during reproduction.

Plasmids

Structure: Small, circular DNA molecules that are separate from the main chromosome. Bacteria may have zero, one, or many plasmids.

Function: Often carry genes for antibiotic resistance, toxin production, or other advantageous traits. Plasmids can be transferred between bacteria, spreading these traits through populations. This is why antibiotic resistance can spread rapidly.

Ribosomes

Structure: Small granular structures made of RNA and protein. Prokaryotic ribosomes are 70S (smaller than eukaryotic 80S ribosomes). They have no membrane.

Function: The site of protein synthesis. Ribosomes read the genetic code from mRNA and assemble amino acids into proteins. A single bacterial cell may contain thousands of ribosomes.

Flagella and Pili

Flagella: Long, whip-like projections made of the protein flagellin. They rotate like propellers to move the bacterium. Not all bacteria have flagella.

Pili (singular: pilus): Short, hair-like projections. Used for attachment to surfaces and to other bacteria during conjugation (transfer of genetic material between bacteria).

Worked Example: Identifying Prokaryotic Structures

Question: A student observes a cell with the following features: circular DNA without a membrane, 70S ribosomes, a cell wall containing peptidoglycan, and no mitochondria. Is this cell prokaryotic or eukaryotic? Justify your answer.

Step 1: Identify the key features

  • Circular DNA without a membrane = nucleoid (no true nucleus)
  • 70S ribosomes = prokaryotic (eukaryotes have 80S)
  • Peptidoglycan cell wall = characteristic of bacteria
  • No mitochondria = prokaryotic characteristic

Step 2: Compare to cell characteristics

Prokaryotic cells lack a membrane-bound nucleus, have 70S ribosomes, possess cell walls (often with peptidoglycan in bacteria), and lack membrane-bound organelles like mitochondria. All features match.

Answer:

This cell is prokaryotic, specifically a bacterium. The circular DNA without a surrounding membrane indicates a nucleoid rather than a true nucleus. The 70S ribosomes are characteristic of prokaryotes, as eukaryotes have larger 80S ribosomes. The peptidoglycan cell wall is unique to bacteria. The absence of mitochondria further confirms this, as prokaryotes perform cellular respiration across their plasma membrane instead.

Prokaryotic Reproduction: Binary Fission

Prokaryotes reproduce asexually through a process called binary fission. This is a simple and rapid form of cell division that produces two genetically identical daughter cells.

Key Definition

Binary fission: A type of asexual reproduction where a prokaryotic cell divides into two identical daughter cells. "Binary" means two, and "fission" means splitting.

Steps of Binary Fission

1

DNA Replication

The circular chromosome replicates, producing two identical copies of the DNA.

2

Cell Elongation

The cell grows longer. The two copies of the chromosome move to opposite ends of the cell.

3

Septum Formation

New plasma membrane and cell wall material grow inward at the middle of the cell, forming a septum (dividing wall).

4

Cell Division

The septum closes completely, and the two daughter cells separate. Each daughter cell is genetically identical to the parent cell.

Binary fission is extremely rapid. Under optimal conditions, E. coli can divide every 20 minutes. This means a single bacterium could theoretically produce over 4 billion descendants in just 12 hours! This rapid reproduction allows bacteria to quickly adapt to changing environments through natural selection.

Bacteria vs Archaea

Although bacteria and archaea are both prokaryotes, they are as different from each other as they are from eukaryotes. In fact, archaea are more closely related to eukaryotes in some ways than to bacteria.

Feature Bacteria Archaea
Cell Wall Contains peptidoglycan No peptidoglycan (pseudopeptidoglycan or protein)
Membrane Lipids Ester-linked phospholipids Ether-linked lipids (more stable)
Habitats Found everywhere; soil, water, organisms Many in extreme environments; also soil, ocean, gut
Human Pathogens Many species cause disease No known human pathogens
Antibiotic Sensitivity Affected by many antibiotics Resistant to most antibiotics (different cell wall)

Extremophile Archaea

Some archaea thrive in environments that would kill most other organisms:

  • Thermophiles: Live in extremely hot environments (60-120Â°C), like hot springs and hydrothermal vents
  • Halophiles: Live in extremely salty environments, like salt lakes and salt flats
  • Methanogens: Produce methane gas; live in oxygen-free environments like swamps, cow stomachs, and sewage
  • Acidophiles: Thrive in acidic conditions (pH below 5)

Short Answer Marking Guidelines

Question 6 (6 marks):

1 mark for each correctly drawn and labelled structure (max 6): plasma membrane, cell wall, nucleoid, ribosomes, cytoplasm, flagella, pili, capsule, plasmid. Diagram should show appropriate relative positions (e.g., cell wall outside membrane, nucleoid region in cytoplasm).

Question 7 (3 marks):

Antibiotics target peptidoglycan synthesis (1 mark). Bacteria have peptidoglycan in cell walls (1 mark). Archaea do not have peptidoglycan - they have different cell wall components (1 mark). Therefore, these antibiotics cannot affect archaeal cells.

Question 8 (5 marks):

Binary fission steps (3 marks): DNA replication; cell elongation with chromosomes moving apart; septum formation; division into two identical daughter cells.
Rapid adaptation (2 marks): Fast reproduction rate (can be 20 min); mutations create genetic variation; large populations mean more variation; natural selection selects advantageous traits; beneficial mutations spread quickly.

Question 9 (6 marks):

Similarities (1 mark each, max 3): Both prokaryotes; no membrane-bound nucleus; no membrane-bound organelles; have plasma membrane; have ribosomes; have circular DNA; unicellular; reproduce by binary fission.
Differences (1 mark each, max 3): Cell wall composition (peptidoglycan vs no peptidoglycan); membrane lipids (ester-linked vs ether-linked); some archaea are extremophiles; no known archaeal human pathogens; different antibiotic sensitivity; different RNA polymerase structure.
