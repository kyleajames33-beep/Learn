Think About This...

Your body contains approximately 37 trillion cells - that's 37,000,000,000,000 individual living units working together right now. Each cell is a self-contained factory, taking in nutrients, producing energy, and carrying out specific functions. A single drop of your blood contains about 5 million red blood cells, each one completing a full circuit of your body in just 20 seconds. How do trillions of microscopic cells coordinate to keep you alive?

What is a Cell?

A cell is the smallest structural and functional unit of life. All living organisms are made of cells - from the simplest bacteria to complex organisms like humans. Cells are often called the "building blocks of life" because they are the basic units from which all living things are constructed.

Key Definition

Cell: The basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms. A cell is the smallest unit that can carry out all the processes necessary for life, including metabolism, growth, reproduction, and response to stimuli.

Every cell shares certain fundamental characteristics. All cells are surrounded by a plasma membrane (also called the cell membrane) that separates the cell's internal environment from its external surroundings. Inside every cell is cytoplasm, a gel-like substance where chemical reactions occur. All cells also contain genetic material (DNA) that stores the instructions for the cell's activities and allows the cell to reproduce.

All Cells Have

  • Plasma membrane (cell membrane)
  • Cytoplasm
  • Genetic material (DNA)
  • Ribosomes for protein synthesis

All Cells Can

  • Obtain and use energy (metabolism)
  • Respond to their environment
  • Grow and develop
  • Reproduce (make copies of themselves)

Why Must Cells Be Small?

Most cells are microscopic, typically ranging from 1 to 100 micrometres (μm) in diameter. A micrometre is one-millionth of a metre (0.000001 m). This small size isn't accidental - it's essential for cell survival. The reason relates to the surface area to volume ratio.

The Surface Area to Volume Problem

As a cell increases in size, its volume increases faster than its surface area. This creates a problem: the cell membrane (surface area) is responsible for exchanging materials with the environment, while the cytoplasm (volume) contains all the processes that need those materials.

If a cell becomes too large, its surface area won't be sufficient to supply nutrients to, or remove waste from, its large volume. The cell would essentially "starve" or be poisoned by its own waste products. Small cells have a high surface area to volume ratio, allowing efficient exchange of materials.

Some cells, like nerve cells (neurons), solve this problem by being very long but thin. Others, like red blood cells, have a flattened disc shape to maximise their surface area. The largest single cells, like ostrich eggs, are mostly yolk (stored nutrients) with very little active cytoplasm.

Worked Example: Calculating Surface Area to Volume Ratio

Question: Compare the surface area to volume ratio of a cube-shaped cell with sides of 1 μm versus a cube with sides of 4 μm.

Step 1: Calculate for the small cube (1 μm sides)

Surface Area = 6 × (1)² = 6 μm²
Volume = (1)³ = 1 μm³
SA:V ratio = 6:1 or 6

Step 2: Calculate for the large cube (4 μm sides)

Surface Area = 6 × (4)² = 96 μm²
Volume = (4)³ = 64 μm³
SA:V ratio = 96:64 = 1.5:1 or 1.5

Conclusion:

The smaller cell has a SA:V ratio of 6, while the larger cell has a ratio of only 1.5. This means the small cell has 4 times more surface area relative to its volume, allowing it to exchange materials much more efficiently. This is why cells must remain small.

Unicellular vs Multicellular Organisms

Organisms can be classified based on the number of cells they contain. Unicellular organisms consist of a single cell that performs all life functions. Multicellular organisms are made of many cells that work together, often with specialised functions.

Unicellular Organisms

The entire organism is a single cell. This one cell must perform all functions necessary for life: obtaining nutrients, producing energy, removing waste, responding to stimuli, and reproducing.

Examples: Bacteria (e.g., E. coli), Archaea, many protists (e.g., Amoeba, Paramecium), some fungi (yeasts)

Multicellular Organisms

The organism contains many cells. Different cells become specialised for specific functions (cell differentiation), allowing for greater complexity and efficiency.

Examples: Animals (including humans), plants, most fungi, some protists (e.g., seaweed)

Advantages of Multicellularity

  • Cell specialisation: Cells can become highly efficient at specific tasks
  • Larger body size: Allows organisms to exploit different ecological niches
  • Longer lifespan: Individual cells can be replaced while the organism survives
  • Greater complexity: Enables the development of organs and organ systems

The Diversity of Cell Types

While all cells share fundamental features, there is remarkable diversity in cell structure. Scientists classify cells into two main categories: prokaryotic cells and eukaryotic cells.

Prokaryotic Cells

Simple cells without a nucleus or membrane-bound organelles. Their DNA floats freely in the cytoplasm in a region called the nucleoid.

Include: Bacteria and Archaea
Size: Usually 0.1-5 μm
Examples: E. coli, Staphylococcus

Eukaryotic Cells

Complex cells with a true nucleus (containing DNA) and membrane-bound organelles like mitochondria, endoplasmic reticulum, and Golgi apparatus.

Include: Animals, plants, fungi, protists
Size: Usually 10-100 μm
Examples: Human cells, plant cells

We will explore both prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells in detail in the following lessons. Understanding the differences between these cell types is fundamental to biology and helps explain the diversity of life on Earth.
